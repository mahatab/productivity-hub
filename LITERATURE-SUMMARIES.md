### Hans Christian Andersen - Fairy Tales (Complete Collection - 168 Tales)

**Overview:** Danish author (1805-1875) who wrote 168 fairy tales, many darker and more philosophical than modern adaptations suggest.

**Major Themes:**
- Mortality and redemption
- Social class and inequality
- Vanity and pride
- Spiritual/religious undertones
- The outsider/misfit finding their place

**Notable Stories:**

**The Little Mermaid**
- Mermaid sacrifices voice for legs to win prince's love
- Prince marries another; mermaid must kill him or die
- She chooses death, becomes "daughter of air" earning soul through good deeds
- Theme: Unrequited love, sacrifice, spiritual redemption

**The Ugly Duckling**
- "Ugly" bird bullied and rejected by all
- Transforms into beautiful swan
- Theme: Finding where you belong, inner beauty

**The Emperor's New Clothes**
- Con artists "weave" invisible clothes for vain emperor
- Only child has courage to say "He's naked!"
- Theme: Groupthink, vanity, truth-telling

**The Snow Queen**
- Gerda's quest to rescue Kai from Snow Queen's ice palace
- Love and warmth overcome cold logic
- Theme: Love conquers evil, friendship

**The Little Match Girl**
- Poor girl freezing, selling matches on New Year's Eve
- Visions of warmth and grandmother in match flames
- Dies in the cold but goes to heaven
- Theme: Poverty, social injustice, death as release

**The Nightingale**
- Emperor prefers mechanical bird to real nightingale
- When dying, only real nightingale can save him
- Theme: Authenticity vs artificiality, nature vs technology

**The Red Shoes**
- Vain girl's cursed shoes force her to dance endlessly
- Must have feet cut off to stop
- Theme: Vanity, punishment, redemption

**Thumbelina (Little Tiny)**
- Tiny girl born from flower
- Adventures with toad, mole, finally marries flower prince
- Theme: Finding your place, staying true to yourself

**Deeper/Lesser-Known Tales:**

**Anne Lisbeth**
- Woman abandons her son, nurses nobleman's child
- Son drowns, haunts her as ghost
- Psychological horror about guilt and redemption

**The Shadow**
- Man's shadow gains independence, becomes more successful
- Shadow takes over, kills original master
- Theme: Doppelganger, identity, evil winning

**The Girl Who Trod on the Loaf**
- Proud girl steps on bread to avoid mud
- Sinks into hell, turned to statue
- Eventually redeemed through repentance
- Theme: Pride, waste, redemption

**Literary Significance:**
- Influenced modern fairy tale tradition
- Blend of folk tales and original creations
- Pioneered using fairy tales for social commentary
- Often used personal pain in stories (unrequited love, feeling like outsider)

## 🔄 IN-PROGRESS WORKS

### Arthur Conan Doyle - The Adventures of Sherlock Holmes (1892)

**Status:** 2 of 12 stories read

**Overview:** First collection of Sherlock Holmes short stories featuring the detective and Dr. Watson solving mysteries in Victorian London.

**Stories Completed:**

#### 1. A Scandal in Bohemia
**Plot:** King of Bohemia hires Holmes to retrieve compromising photograph from Irene Adler before his wedding. She outsmarts Holmes and escapes with husband but promises not to use photo.

**Key Points:**
- Only woman to beat Holmes
- Holmes keeps her photo as memento
- "To Sherlock Holmes she is always THE woman"
- Theme: Intelligence and wit over brute force
- Irene Adler becomes iconic character

**Famous Quotes:**
- "To Sherlock Holmes she is always the woman. I have seldom heard him mention her under any other name."
- Holmes's deductive method: examining mud on shoes, ink stains on fingers

**Characters:**
- Irene Adler: Opera singer, intelligent, resourceful
- King of Bohemia: Wilhelm Gottsreich Sigismond von Ormstein
- Godfrey Norton: Irene's husband, lawyer

#### 2. The Red-Headed League (In Progress)
**Setup:** Red-haired pawnbroker hired for mysterious well-paying job copying encyclopedia. League suddenly dissolved. Holmes investigates.

**Signature Holmes Elements:**
- Deductive reasoning from small details
- Disguises and subterfuge
- Watson as narrator and everyman
- Victorian London atmosphere
- "Elementary, my dear Watson" approach

**Writing Style:**
- Watson narrates retrospectively
- Mystery unfolds gradually
- Holmes explains logic at end
- Combines action with cerebral puzzle-solving

### Jane Austen - Pride and Prejudice (1813)

**Status:** First 4 chapters read

**Setting:** English countryside, Regency era, Bennet family estate Longbourn

**Main Characters:**
- **Elizabeth Bennet:** Second eldest, intelligent, witty, "fine eyes"
- **Mr. Fitzwilliam Darcy:** Wealthy, proud, initially rude, estate at Pemberley
- **Jane Bennet:** Eldest, beautiful, sweet-tempered
- **Mr. Bingley:** Wealthy, friendly, rents Netherfield Park
- **Mrs. Bennet:** Mother obsessed with marrying off daughters, neurotic
- **Mr. Bennet:** Father, witty, ironic, detached from family chaos
- **Lydia Bennet:** Youngest, flirtatious, reckless
- **Mr. Collins:** Pompous cousin, will inherit Longbourn (entailed to male heir)
- **Lady Catherine de Bourgh:** Darcy's wealthy, overbearing aunt

**Plot So Far:**
- Bingley rents Netherfield, brings friend Darcy
- Netherfield ball: Bingley dances with Jane twice (scandal!)
- Darcy refuses to dance with Elizabeth: "She is tolerable, but not handsome enough to tempt me"
- Elizabeth overhears, finds it amusing rather than offensive
- Mrs. Bennet schemes to get daughters married
- Mr. Bennet's dry wit vs Mrs. Bennet's hysteria

**Famous Opening:**
"It is a truth universally acknowledged, that a single man in possession of a good fortune must be in want of a wife."

**Themes:**
- Marriage and social class
- First impressions vs true character
- Pride and prejudice (both Elizabeth and Darcy guilty)
- Women's limited options in Regency England
- Individual vs family reputation

**Austen's Style:**
- Free indirect discourse (narrator blends with character thoughts)
- Irony and wit
- Social satire
- Limited omniscient narrator
- Dialogue reveals character

**Historical Context:**
- Women couldn't inherit property (entailment)
- Marriage = economic survival for women
- Social mobility very limited
- "Accomplished" ladies: music, drawing, French, needlework
- Balls and visiting = social life

### Charles Dickens - A Tale of Two Cities (1859)

**Status:** Opening chapters (Book 1) read

**Setting:** London and Paris during French Revolution (1775-1793)

**Famous Opening:**
"It was the best of times, it was the worst of times, it was the age of wisdom, it was the age of foolishness, it was the epoch of belief, it was the epoch of incredulity, it was the season of Light, it was the season of Darkness, it was the spring of hope, it was the winter of despair..."

**Main Characters Introduced:**
- **Jarvis Lorry:** Tellson's Bank employee, proper, methodical
- **Dr. Alexandre Manette:** French doctor imprisoned 18 years in Bastille
- **Lucie Manette:** Dr. Manette's daughter, raised in England thinking father dead
- **Jerry Cruncher:** Messenger, mysterious "resurrection man"

**Plot So Far:**
- 1775: Mr. Lorry receives mysterious message: "Recalled to Life"
- Travels to Dover to meet Lucie Manette (17 years old)
- Reveals her father alive, imprisoned 18 years, recently released
- Going to Paris to retrieve him from old servant's house
- Lucie shocked: "I am going to see his Ghost!"

**Atmosphere:**
- Fog, darkness, suspicion everywhere
- Dover mail coach: passengers suspicious of each other (highwaymen everywhere)
- Revolutionary tensions brewing in France
- England also violent (hangings, robberies)

**Dickens's Imagery:**
- Woodman (Fate) and Farmer (Death) preparing Revolution's guillotine
- Shadows and secrets ("every human creature is a profound secret")
- Resurrection theme (Dr. Manette "recalled to life")

**Themes Emerging:**
- Resurrection and renewal
- Secrets and hidden identity
- Violence and revolution
- Sacrifice
- Doubles and parallels (two cities)

**Historical Background:**
- French Revolution (1789-1799)
- Bastille prison symbol of oppression
- Tellson's Bank: old, conservative, represents stability
- Aristocratic privilege vs common people

### Mary Shelley - Frankenstein (1818)

**Status:** First chapters read (Walton's letters + Victor's childhood)

**Subtitle:** "The Modern Prometheus"

**Structure:** Frame narrative within frame narrative
1. Walton's letters to sister Margaret
2. Victor's story told to Walton
3. (Later: Creature's story told to Victor)

**Characters Introduced:**

**Robert Walton:**
- Arctic explorer seeking North Pole passage
- Writes letters to sister Margaret Saville
- Lonely, seeks friend/equal
- Finds Victor Frankenstein on ice floe

**Victor Frankenstein:**
- Born in Geneva to wealthy family
- Parents: Alphonse (older, noble) and Caroline Beaufort
- Adopted sister Elizabeth Lavenza (golden-haired, angelic)
- Best friend: Henry Clerval (romantic, chivalric)
- Obsessed with alchemy and "secrets of nature"

**Plot So Far:**

**Walton's Arctic Expedition:**
- Sees giant creature on sledge across ice
- Next day rescues Victor, emaciated and frozen
- Victor pursuing the creature
- Victor warns Walton against dangerous ambition
- Begins telling his tale

**Victor's Childhood:**
- Idyllic upbringing in Geneva
- Parents find orphan Elizabeth, raise her as Victor's future wife
- "I have a pretty present for my Victor"
- Elizabeth becomes "more than sister"
- Happy family life with younger brother William

**Victor's Fateful Discovery:**
- Age 13, finds Cornelius Agrippa (medieval alchemist)
- Father dismisses it as "sad trash" but doesn't explain why
- Victor studies Agrippa, Paracelsus, Albertus Magnus in secret
- Fascinated by occult, transmutation, immortality

**Themes:**
- Dangerous knowledge
- Playing God
- Ambition and obsession
- Isolation and friendship
- Nature vs science
- Creation and responsibility

**Gothic Elements:**
- Arctic setting (sublime, dangerous)
- Mysterious creature
- Pursuit and obsession
- Death and reanimation
- Transgressive science

**Shelley's Background:**
- Written 1816 (age 18) at Lord Byron's villa
- Ghost story competition
- Influenced by galvanism (electricity animating corpses)
- Personal tragedy: multiple infant deaths
- Romantic era: emotion, nature, individual
- But also cautionary about Romantic overreach

**Literary Significance:**
- Often called first science fiction novel
- Questions limits of science
- Creature becomes cultural icon
- Explores what makes us human

### Robert Louis Stevenson - Treasure Island (1883)

**Status:** First chapters read (Part One - The Old Buccaneer)

**Setting:** 18th century England, Admiral Benbow inn on the coast

**Narrator:** Jim Hawkins (young boy, retrospective narration)

**Characters Introduced:**

**Billy Bones (The Captain):**
- Old seaman with saber scar on cheek
- Sings "Fifteen men on the dead man's chest - Yo-ho-ho, and a bottle of rum!"
- Pays Jim to watch for "seafaring man with one leg"
- Former first mate to Captain Flint
- Holds secret treasure map

**Jim Hawkins:**
- Boy whose parents run Admiral Benbow inn
- Brave, resourceful, narrator
- Caught in pirate conspiracy

**Black Dog:**
- Pale, missing two fingers
- Former shipmate of Billy Bones
- Fight with captain, escapes wounded

**Blind Pew:**
- Terrifying blind beggar
- Delivers "Black Spot" (pirate death summons)
- Billy dies of stroke after receiving it

**Dr. Livesey:**
- Calm, authoritative doctor/magistrate
- Stands up to Billy Bones
- Later becomes key ally

**Squire Trelawney:**
- Mentioned as Jim's patron (full role to come)

**Plot So Far:**

**Billy Bones Arrives:**
- Takes room at Admiral Benbow
- Paranoid, watches for enemies
- Drinks rum excessively
- Violent when drunk, tells terrifying pirate stories
- Father too afraid to demand payment

**Black Dog's Visit:**
- Mysterious visitor looking for "mate Bill"
- Fight between Black Dog and Billy
- Billy has stroke after Black Dog escapes

**The Black Spot:**
- Blind Pew arrives during father's final illness
- Forces Jim to take him to captain
- Delivers Black Spot: "You have till ten tonight"
- Billy dies of apoplectic stroke

**The Sea Chest:**
- Father dies, Jim and mother alone
- Must search Billy's chest before pirates return
- Find treasure map in oilcloth packet!
- Hear pirates approaching
- Escape just in time with map

**Treasure Map:**
- Captain Flint's treasure location
- Pirates desperately seeking it
- Jim has it - adventure begins!

**Themes:**
- Coming of age
- Adventure and danger
- Greed and treasure
- Courage vs cowardice
- Loyalty and betrayal

**Atmosphere:**
- Fog, darkness, isolation
- Constant tension and fear
- Pirates closing in
- Boy thrust into adult dangers

**Famous Elements:**
- "Fifteen men on the dead man's chest"
- Black Spot
- Treasure map
- One-legged pirate (Long John Silver - not yet appeared)
- "Pieces of eight"

**Stevenson's Style:**
- First-person retrospective (Jim as adult looking back)
- Fast-paced action
- Vivid descriptive language
- Suspense and cliffhangers
- Accessible to young and adult readers
